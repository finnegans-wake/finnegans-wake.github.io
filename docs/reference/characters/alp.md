# ALP: Anna Livia Plurabelle

Anna Livia Plurabelle is HCE's wife, the mother of the Earwicker children, and the personification of the River Liffey. She is the "eternal feminine"—life force, water, time, and renewal.

## Quick Reference

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Role | Mother, wife, river, defender |
| Sigil | Δ (delta/triangle) |
| Husband | Humphrey Chimpden Earwicker (HCE) |
| Children | Shem, Shaun, Issy |
| Key Chapters | I.5 (the letter), I.8 (washerwomen), IV (monologue) |
| Key Symbol | The River Liffey |

---

## The Name

**Anna Livia Plurabelle** encodes multiple meanings:

| Element | Meaning |
|---------|---------|
| **Anna** | Grace; also a palindrome (reflecting the book's circularity) |
| **Livia** | The River Liffey (Latin: *Abhainn na Life*) |
| **Plurabelle** | "Many beautiful" (Latin *plura* + French *belle*) |

The name suggests: **many beautiful things flowing through time**.

## ALP as River

ALP is identified with the River Liffey that flows through Dublin:

> "Anna was, Livia is, Plurabelle's to be."

As the Liffey, she:
- Flows through HCE (the city/landscape)
- Carries away his guilt
- Connects past to present to future
- Eventually reaches the sea (death) and returns as rain (rebirth)

The famous chapter I.8 ("Anna Livia Plurabelle") weaves **500+ river names** from around the world into the dialogue of the washerwomen.

---

## The Letter

ALP writes a letter defending HCE from the charges against him. This letter:

- Is found in a midden heap by a hen (ALP herself)
- Is analyzed in chapter I.5 ("The Mamafesta")
- Never reaches its intended destination
- May be the Wake itself

The letter is described as:
> "a proteiform graph... a polyhedron of scripture" (p. 107)

---

## The Washerwomen (I.8)

Chapter I.8 is ALP's defining chapter:

- Two washerwomen gossip about ALP on opposite banks of the Liffey
- They wash HCE's dirty laundry (literally and metaphorically)
- As dusk falls, they transform—one into a tree, one into a stone
- The prose is "widely recognized as one of the most beautiful prose-poems in English"

Joyce described it as:
> "a chattering dialogue across the river by two washerwomen who as night falls become a tree and a stone"

---

## The Final Monologue (IV)

ALP speaks the book's final pages (pp. 619–628):

- She tries to wake her sleeping husband
- She remembers their life together
- She flows toward the sea
- Her sentence breaks off, continuing on page 3

This monologue is the most emotionally direct section of the Wake—comparable to the ending of "The Dead" but from a feminine perspective.

### Key Lines
> "Rise up, man of the hooths, you have slept so long!"

> "A way a lone a last a loved a long the"

---

## ALP and Her Children

As mother to Shem, Shaun, and Issy:

- She defends Shem at the end of I.7 (breaking into Shaun's attack)
- She gives gifts to all 111 children (I.8)
- She is the source from which all flow
- She is the destination to which all return

---

## The Hen

ALP appears early in the book as a hen ("Biddy") who scratches in the midden heap and discovers the buried letter. The hen is:

- A "gnarlybird"
- A scavenger
- A connector of past to present
- ALP in animal form

The hen's discovery of the letter in the kitchen midden parallels ALP's creation of the letter in defense of HCE.

## The Delta Symbol (Δ)

Joyce's sigil for ALP is the triangle or delta (Δ):

- The shape of a river's mouth (delta)
- The female pubic triangle (fertility)
- The letter "A" (her initial)
- Stability and flow combined

---

## ALP vs. HCE

| ALP | HCE |
|-----|-----|
| River | Mountain/City |
| Flowing | Fixed |
| Redeeming | Guilty |
| Speaking | Silent (mostly) |
| Final word | First word |

They are complementary opposites—he is the city, she is the river that flows through it.

## Joyce's Recording

In August 1929, Joyce recorded himself reading from the "Anna Livia Plurabelle" chapter—the **only recording** of Joyce reading from *Finnegans Wake*.

Sylvia Beach recalled:
> "How beautiful the 'Anna Livia' recording is, and how amusing Joyce's rendering of an Irish washerwoman's brogue!"

**Find it**: Search "Joyce Anna Livia Plurabelle recording" on YouTube.

## Reading ALP

### Tips
1. **Watch for A-L-P patterns** — Her initials appear like HCE's
2. **Listen for water** — River imagery signals her presence
3. **Note the voice** — She speaks more directly than other characters
4. **Feel the flow** — Her sections have a musical, liquid quality
